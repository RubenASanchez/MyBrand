Introduction


What exactly is a journal article annotator or organizer?


Well, it really depends on who you ask. For a professor or researcher who has been around for a very, very, very long time it may well be a filing cabinet filled with printed out readers covered in post it notes. Or it may be a collection of carboard boxes containing articles organized by author or subject. For a brand new grad student it may be stacks of papers piled on their desk and covered with a collage of colorful highlights and shaky caffeinated scribbled notes.


But the underlying similarity is that they are a system for organizing the articles you are collecting as well as the notes you are writting for those articles.


Luckily, we have computers and cloud storage to help us manage and organize our collection of articles and notes. We can easily create folders in our laptop and store electronic copies of our articles. This is nice since we can organize these files as we see fit. We can also create Word docs or Google docs for all of our notes and annotations. The downside is that although this system does organize the articles and notes, it is actually two systems of organization. Even if the journals and notes are kept in the same folder, this system can become very difficult to search through as the number of articles and notes grows. Do not despair, many companies are recognizing our need for software to help us stay on top of our busy academic, professional, and personal lives. As a matter of fact, many of you have already been using some of these products.


Software products such as Evernote and OneNote can be used to organize all your notes and annotiations, althougth they do this very differently from each other. DropBox and Google Drive are great for storing your PDF article collections. Most laptops come preinstalled with PDF readers which are actually getting better at adding annotations and notes directly to the PDF. But there is still a problem. For these products there is very little if any integration between them. You would still have to search for your articles and notes in different filing systems as well as using different user interfaces and having to remember multiple passwords. Wouldn't it be great if there was a system in place that integrated these multiple functionalities into one product? Good news! There is. Actually, there are.


Mostly targeted to academics and researchers there exist a number of products which strive to integrate article organization and article annotations. They succeed in this integration to varying degrees. And what they wish to integrate varies just as much. I have chosen to provide you with a review of 4 products in order to give you a balanced sense of what has been classically available and what is new and contemporary.
